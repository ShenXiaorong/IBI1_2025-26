Findings and interpretation
The human DLX5 protein is 289 amino acids long and is localised in the nucleus. In the online BLAST results, the reported percentage identities ranged from 52.3% to 100%, showing that DLX5 is highly conserved in some organisms but less similar in more distant sequences.
Using the Python alignment script, three pairwise comparisons were performed. The human–mouse comparison gave an alignment score of 10 and a percentage identity of 96.54%. The human–random comparison gave an alignment score of 276 and a percentage identity of 4.50%. The mouse–random comparison gave an alignment score of 276 and a percentage identity of 4.50%.
The human and mouse DLX5 sequences were the most closely related because they had the lowest alignment score and the highest percentage identity. This means that they had the fewest amino acid differences across the aligned sequence. In contrast, the comparisons involving the random sequence showed lower percentage identity and higher alignment scores, indicating that the similarity between human and mouse DLX5 is not due to chance. This supports the idea that DLX5 is evolutionarily conserved between human and mouse.
